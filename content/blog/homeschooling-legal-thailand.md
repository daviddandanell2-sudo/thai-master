# Is Homeschooling Legal in Thailand? A Definitive Guide for Parents

If you're considering homeschooling your child in Thailand, the first question you need answered is simple: is it legal? The short answer is yes — homeschooling is explicitly recognized under Thai law. But the practical reality involves registration requirements, curriculum choices, and access to examinations that every parent should understand before making the decision.

## The Legal Basis: What Thai Law Says

Homeschooling is explicitly recognized under the **Thai Constitution and the National Education Act of 1999** (also known as the National Education Act B.E. 2542). This legislation established that education can take place outside the formal school system, provided it meets certain standards.

Under the National Education Act, parents who wish to homeschool must register with either:

- **OBEC** (Office of the Basic Education Commission) — the primary route for most homeschooling families
- **OPEC** (Office of the Private Education Commission) — for families following alternative or international curricula

Registration is not optional. Homeschooling without registration is not legally protected, and families who fail to register may face questions from local education authorities.

## Registration Requirements

### Who Can Register

Parents or legal guardians can register to homeschool their children. The requirements vary slightly by province, but generally include:

- Proof of the child's identity (passport or Thai ID card)
- Parent/guardian identification
- A proposed curriculum or educational plan
- Evidence that the home environment is suitable for education

### The Curriculum Plan

When registering, parents must submit a curriculum plan. This does not need to follow the Thai national curriculum exactly, but it must demonstrate that the child will receive a comprehensive education covering core subjects. Many homeschooling families in Thailand use:

- **International curricula** — Cambridge (IGCSE, A Level), IB, or American programmes
- **British National Curriculum** — commonly used by expat families
- **Mixed approaches** — combining structured curricula with project-based learning
- **Worldschooling** — learning through travel and real-world experiences, often supplemented with structured materials

The key requirement is that the curriculum plan shows clear educational objectives and a path to assessment.

### Assessment and Reporting

Registered homeschooling families are typically required to submit periodic reports on their child's progress. The exact frequency and format depend on the registering office, but most families report annually or semi-annually. These reports should include:

- Evidence of learning (work samples, portfolios, test results)
- Progress against the stated curriculum plan
- Plans for the upcoming period

## Examination Access: The Critical Challenge

One of the most important practical considerations for homeschooling families in Thailand is access to examinations. If your child is following an international curriculum like IGCSE, A Level, or IB, they will need to sit formal exams to gain qualifications.

### IGCSE and A Level Exam Centres

The **British Council in Bangkok** operates Cambridge exam centres where homeschooled students can register to sit IGCSE and A Level examinations. This is the primary route for families following the British or Cambridge curriculum.

Key points:
- Registration is typically done through the British Council website or by contacting their exams team directly
- Exam sessions run in May/June and October/November
- Fees vary by subject but expect to pay approximately THB 8,500 – 9,700 per IGCSE subject
- Students must register well in advance of exam deadlines

For families outside Bangkok, traveling to exam centres is a practical consideration. Some families plan exam trips around the May/June and November sessions, combining exam sittings with visits to Bangkok.

### IB Examinations

IB examinations are more complex for homeschoolers because the IB Diploma is designed as a school-based programme. Homeschooled students cannot typically sit IB exams as independent candidates. Families wishing to follow the IB usually need to:

- Enroll in an IB World School as a part-time or distance student (where the school allows this)
- Use an accredited online IB programme
- Consider alternative qualifications (A Levels or Cambridge International A Levels) that are more accessible to independent candidates

### SAT and ACT

For families considering university in the United States, SAT and ACT test centres are available in Bangkok, Chiang Mai, and other major cities. Registration is done directly through the College Board (SAT) or ACT websites.

## The Homeschooling Community in Thailand

Thailand has an active homeschooling community, particularly in:

- **Chiang Mai** — one of the most popular locations in Asia for worldschooling and homeschooling families
- **Bangkok** — a large community with active co-ops and support groups
- **Phuket** — a growing community of long-stay families

### Community Resources

- **Chiang Mai Homeschool Co-op** — offers classes, field trips, and social events
- **Grace Connect at Grace International School** — provides curriculum resources and support (note: this is support, not a complete programme)
- **Worldschooling hubs** — organized groups that travel together or meet in specific locations
- **Online communities** — Facebook groups and forums where Thai homeschooling families share resources and advice

## Challenges of Homeschooling in Thailand

### Language
While homeschooling is legal, much of the registration process and official communication is in Thai. Expat families may need translation support or assistance from Thai-speaking contacts.

### Socialization
The most common concern parents raise about homeschooling is socialization. In Thailand, this is mitigated by:
- Active homeschool co-ops and groups
- Extracurricular activities (sports, music, arts)
- Worldschooling communities
- Part-time enrollment in some international school programmes

### University Recognition
Universities in Thailand and internationally generally recognize IGCSE and A Level qualifications obtained by homeschooled students, provided exams are taken at accredited centres. For Thai universities, specific requirements vary, and families should check with individual institutions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is homeschooling really legal in Thailand?**
Yes. Homeschooling is explicitly recognized under the National Education Act of 1999. Parents must register with OBEC or OPEC.

**Do I need to follow the Thai curriculum?**
No. You must submit a curriculum plan, but it can be an international curriculum such as Cambridge, British, or American.

**Can my child take IGCSE exams if they're homeschooled?**
Yes. Homeschooled students can register for Cambridge IGCSE and A Level exams through the British Council in Bangkok.

**Can my child take IB exams if they're homeschooled?**
Generally no. The IB Diploma is designed as a school-based programme. Alternative qualifications like A Levels are more accessible.

**How do I register to homeschool?**
Contact OBEC or OPEC with your child's identification, your identification, and a proposed curriculum plan. Requirements vary slightly by province.

**Do I need to speak Thai to homeschool in Thailand?**
No, but registration paperwork may be in Thai. Many expat families work with Thai-speaking contacts or agents to complete registration.

## Making the Decision

Homeschooling in Thailand is legal, supported by an active community, and practical for families who plan carefully. The key requirements are:

1. Register with OBEC or OPEC
2. Develop a comprehensive curriculum plan
3. Arrange examination access (British Council for Cambridge exams)
4. Build a support network (co-ops, online communities, tutors)
5. Plan for socialization and extracurricular activities
